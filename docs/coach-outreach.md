# Talking to coaches

The goal of these conversations is **not** to sell Gardnr. It's to find out whether
the first three lines of the landing page are true.

That page currently tells coaches, with total confidence, that their week is *"food
screenshots buried in three message threads"* and *"rebuilding the same compliance
spreadsheet."* That came from desk research, not from a coach's mouth. If it's
right, these conversations hand you your first users and your first real quotes.
If it's wrong, you find out in a week instead of in six months.

**Both outcomes are wins. Only silence is a loss.**

---

## The rule that governs everything

> **You do not get a coach to try your app by asking them to try your app.**
> You get them to try it by asking questions that make them describe their problem
> so vividly that *they* ask *you* what you'd do about it.

The moment you pitch, they turn polite. Polite coaches say *"that's cool, send me
a link"* — and never open it. **"That's cool" is the sound of a conversation you
have already lost.**

Two hard rules, both from Rob Fitzpatrick's *The Mom Test*, and both violated by
almost every founder:

1. **Ask about their past, never their future.** "Would you use a tool that…" is
   worthless — everyone says yes to be nice. "What did you actually do last
   Sunday?" cannot be faked.
2. **Talk less.** If you are talking more than a third of the time, you are
   pitching, and you are learning nothing.

---

## The opener

Your single biggest asset here is the thing you thought was a weakness: **you are
not a coach.** That makes them the expert and you the student, and people find it
very hard to refuse a student. It is also true, which means you never have to
maintain it.

> Hey [name] — slightly odd ask.
>
> I'm a developer, not a coach. I spent the last couple of months building a
> client-tracking tool for nutrition coaches, and I've realised I built it on
> assumptions rather than on talking to anyone who does the job.
>
> Could I borrow 15 minutes to hear how check-ins actually work for you? I'm not
> pitching you — I genuinely want to know if I got it wrong. Happy to show you
> what I built at the end if you're curious, but that's not why I'm asking.

Why it works: it's honest, it's short, it gives them status, and it explicitly
removes the thing they're bracing for. The line *"I've realised I built it on
assumptions"* is disarming precisely because it's an admission.

**Where they are:** Instagram is where online nutrition coaches actually live —
find the ones posting client check-ins and transformations. Also r/personaltraining
and the online-coaching Facebook groups. Local gyms convert best of all, and are
the most uncomfortable, which is the usual trade.

**Send it to twenty. Talk to five.** Five is enough to see a pattern and small
enough that you'll actually do it. This week — not "soon."

---

## The questions, in order

Ask them exactly like this. Then shut up. The silences are where the truth lives.

**1. How many clients are you coaching right now?**
Factual, easy, and it tells you instantly whether they're your buyer. A coach with
three clients does not need attention triage — they can hold three people in their
head. Your product starts mattering somewhere around ten.

**2. Walk me through your last check-in. What did you actually do to get ready
for it?**
The most important question in the list. Past tense, specific, unfakeable. Do not
help them. Do not fill the pause. Just let them describe it, and write down the
verbs they use.

**3. Between check-ins — how do you know whether they actually stuck to the plan?**
This is Gardnr's entire thesis in one sentence. If the answer is *"I ask them"* or
*"they send me photos"*, you are right. If the answer is *"I use [tool] and it's
fine"*, you have a problem, and you need to know that now.

**4. How long did that take? And how many of those did you do on Sunday?**
Turns pain into a number. Eight clients × 25 minutes is three and a half hours of
unpaid Sunday. Now the pain has a price, and you know what you're allowed to
charge to remove it.

**5. When a client tells you their week went fine — how often does the data agree
with them?**
Your own insight, asked as a question. Every coach has a story here, and the story
they tell you is better marketing copy than anything either of us will ever write.
Write it down verbatim.

**6. Tell me about the last client who went quiet on you. How long before you
noticed?**
This is the triage feature, disguised as a question. If they say *"honestly,
sometimes a couple of weeks"* — that is the wound, and Gardnr is the bandage.

**7. What have you already tried to fix this? What are you paying for right now?**
🔑 **The single most valuable question here.** What somebody already *pays for* is
the only trustworthy evidence that a pain is real. If they pay for TrueCoach or
Practice Better, the pain is real and monetisable. If they've never paid for
anything and run the whole business on WhatsApp and a Google Sheet — ask why, and
listen very carefully to the answer.

**8. Why isn't [that tool] fixing it?**
Their answer is your positioning, in their words, for free. Use it verbatim on the
landing page.

**9. If I could fix one thing about your week, what would it be?**
The one hypothetical you're allowed, and only at the end, because everything before
it was grounded in fact. It tells you what *they* think their problem is — which
may not be what *you* think their problem is. That gap is the whole point.

## The pivot — and when NOT to take it

**If they have not described a pain, do not show them the app.** Thank them, mean
it, and get off the call. Showing a product to somebody who doesn't have the
problem teaches you nothing and burns your one shot at them.

If they *have* bled a little:

> Can I show you something for sixty seconds? I built exactly this, and I want to
> know if I got it wrong.

You are asking them to **critique**, not to buy. That's a far easier yes — and a
coach who is critiquing your product is a coach who is using their imagination on
it.

Then show **one screen**. The triage dashboard. Say one thing:

> *"That's Sam. He's gone quiet for five days. You didn't have to notice."*

Then stop talking. Whatever they say next is the most valuable data of the call.

---

## The ask

Never *"would you like to try it?"* — vague, and trivially easy to defer forever.

Ask for something small, specific, and reversible:

> Pick your most annoying client. The one who never sends their food photos.
>
> Let me set them up — takes me five minutes, costs you nothing, and if it's
> useless by next Sunday, delete it.

Why every piece of that is load-bearing:

- **One client, not their book.** Almost no risk to say yes to.
- **You do the setup.** Zero work for them. Work is where trials die.
- **"Most annoying client."** The one where the pain is sharpest — so the product
  gets its best possible shot at looking like magic.
- **"By next Sunday."** One check-in cycle. A real deadline, not "sometime".
- **"Just delete it."** An explicit escape hatch, which paradoxically makes yes
  much easier.

Then close the loop, and this is the part people forget:

> I'll message you Sunday and we can look at their week together.

That scheduled second touch is what turns a trial into a user. Without it, they
sign up, nothing happens, and you never hear from them again.

## What to listen for

**Green — the pain is real:**
- They get *annoyed* while describing it. Emotion is the signal; words are cheap.
- They volunteer a specific incident you didn't ask for.
- They already pay for something.
- They start asking *"how does it handle…?"* — they're mentally installing it.
- They give you a client's name on the spot.

**Red — the pain isn't what you think:**
- Polite interest with no energy.
- *"That's cool."*
- They cannot name a single specific time this hurt them.
- *"I'd definitely use that!"* … but they won't hand you a client today. Enthusiasm
  that survives no contact with cost is worthless.
- They only have three clients.

**The answer that should make you change the product:**

If most coaches tell you *"my problem isn't seeing compliance — my problem is
getting clients in the first place"*, then you have built an excellent tool for the
wrong bottleneck. Their pain is **sales**, not visibility.

That would be the most valuable thing you learn all year, and it is only learnable
by asking.

---

## Afterwards, every time

Within ten minutes of hanging up, write down:

1. The **exact words** they used for the pain. Not your paraphrase — theirs.
2. What they already pay for, and what it fails to do.
3. Whether they gave you a client. (This is the only score that counts.)

After five conversations, read the five sets of notes side by side. If the same
phrase keeps appearing, **that phrase is your headline** — and it will be better
than the one on the page now, because a coach wrote it.
